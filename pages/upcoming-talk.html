Active Galaxies: Most Powerful Objects in the Universe - An Overview

Active galaxies are highly luminous astrophysical sources in the universe powered by “supermassive black holes” via processes known as “accretion”. They contain a highly bright, compact region at the galaxy’s center called “active galactic nuclei” (AGN). Their emission spreads over a wide range of frequencies through radio to γ-rays. Both thermal and non-thermal radiations from different components of AGN contribute to the total observed emission. They exhibit extreme variations ranging from all the time-scale from minutes to years across the electromagnetic band. These variations occur as flares or quiescent/low-activity states. Studying the cause for such physical properties of AGNs through multi-frequency data using ground and space-based observatories has been found to be one of the most effective ways in the past. Despite such studies in various wavebands, there are several questions for which answers are not fully understood. In this talk, the speaker will briefly introduce AGN and its current understanding status, along with the scope of AGN research.
